23. what phase change has the chocolate undergone​


Crucial to the lives of anyone who like a little indulgence are the 13 Phases: without them the humble cocoa bean could never become a chocolate bar.

Unwrapping a chocolate bar is a simple gesture, but "behind" that gesture are some essential steps, without which there would be no cakes, no chocolates, no hot chocolates and no praline fillings. The long journey that leads from the cocoa bean to the most delicious chocolate is made up of 13 phases.

Phase 1
Cocoa beans are extracted from cabosses (the fruit of the cocoa tree) and cleaned of all foreign material. In fact they are surrounded by a whitish gelatinous substance that must be eliminated.

Phase 2
To bring out the full flavor and color of chocolate, cocoa beans are roasted. The temperature and roasting time changes according to the type of cocoa bean used or the final product to be obtained.

Phase 3
A special machine removes the husk from the beans, leaving only the grains.

Phase 4
Cocoa beans undergo a process of alkalization, usually with the addition of a solution of potassium carbonate. This process brings out the color and typical aroma of cocoa and also gives a fuller flavor, greatly reducing its acid components and astringent properties. By varying the conditions of alkalization cocoa powder is obtained with a widely varying range of pigmentation, from light brown, to reddish, and to dark brown. Usually manufacturers mix more cocoa beans from different origins to obtain a product with a unique aroma and flavor.

Phase 5
The cocoa beans are ground to create the liquor or cocoa mass (namely cocoa particles suspended in cocoa butter).

Phase 6
The cocoa liquor is hot pressed to extract cocoa butter, which is a medium golden colored fluid and what remains is cocoa mass, made up of round, solid particles.

Phase 7
At this point, production follows two paths: cocoa butter, used for chocolate production; and solid mass of cocoa, used to make cocoa powder.

Phase 8
Cocoa liquor (obtained by grinding the beans) is added to cocoa butter (extracted from the liquor itself under high pressure) to make chocolate. Other ingredients, such as sugar, milk, flavoring or emulsifiers, are also added, depending on the blend required.

Phase 9
The mixture undergoes a refining process, passing through a series of rollers until a homogeneous mixture and an improved texture is achieved.

Phase 10
The moment of the conching process, invented in 1879 by Rodolphe Lindt, helps to bring out the fuller flavor and texture of the chocolate. The mass is mixed for hours in a basin to aerate and mix the chocolate, making it harmoniously aromatic.

Phase 11
The mixture is tempered: this means that it is heated, cooled and re-heated until it is perfectly glossy, silky and smooth. This phase prevents the cocoa butter from re-appearing on the surface.

Phase 12
The mixture is put into molds and placed in cooling chambers.

Phase 13
The chocolate is ready to be packaged and sent to the point of sale.

The Cocoa and Chocolate Cluster will be the most indulgent place at Expo Milano 2015. The Pavilion is designed as a metaphorical representation of the jungle: its wooden structures emerge from the ground, taking over the surrounding space, just as in the tropical environments where cocoa is grown.
