Examples of Appositive Gerunds

Some examples of appositive gerunds include "Leslie's passion, cooking," "Stress relievers, such as singing," and "Sociology, studying society,". Appositive gerunds are often in the form of phrases. To better understand what an appositive gerund is, it is fair to discuss what they mean separately first. In this way, this will help in generating examples of appositive gerunds. First, appositive in grammar is a noun or a noun phrase that modifies another noun.

Meanwhile, a gerund is a word in the form of a verb that acts like a noun. Thus, appositive gerunds are nouns or noun phrases in the form of verb but function as nouns. In addition, gerunds act as appositives by giving more information about the noun. Like the first example above, "Leslie's passion" is a noun phrase while "cooking" is a verb in the form of a noun - which is a gerund while the entire phrase functions as an appositive.

19. What is the gerund? ​


Verbals are verbs that act as another part of speech -- either a noun, adjective, or adverb. They are base form of a verb but does not suggest action.

Gerund is a verbal that is derived from a verb but functions as a noun in a sentence.

It is formed by using the base form of the verb + ing

For example:

work + ing = working

study + ing = studying

Gerunds are used in sentences like this:

Swimming is my hobby for almost five years.

In this sentence, swimming is a noun that refers to a hobby.

29. What word comes before the gerund in each sentence? What is the function of the noun or pronoun in relation yo the gerund


The gerund looks exactly the same as a present participle, but it is useful to understand the difference between the two. The gerund always has the same function as a noun.

-> THE GERUND AS THE SUBJECT OF THE SENTENCE

ex. Hunting tigers is dangerous.

-> THE GERUND AS THE COMPLEMENT OF THE VERB 'TO BE'

ex. One of his duties is attending meetings.

-> THE GERUND AFTER PREPOSITIONS

ex. She is good at painting.

-> THE GERUND AFTER PHRASAL VERBS

(Phrasal verbs are composed of a verb + preposition or adverb. )

ex. When will you give up smoking?

-> There are some phrasal verbs that include the word "to" as a preposition for example to look forward to, to take to, to be accustomed to, to get around to, & to be used to. It is important to recognise that the word "to" is a preposition in these cases because it must be followed by a gerund. It is not part of the infinitive form of the verb. You can check whether "to" is a preposition or part of the infinitive. If you can put the pronoun "it" after the word "to" and form a meaningful sentence, then the word "to" is a preposition and must be followed by a gerund.

ex. I look forward to hearing from you soon.

-> THE GERUND IN COMPOUND NOUNS

ex. I am giving Sally a driving lesson.
