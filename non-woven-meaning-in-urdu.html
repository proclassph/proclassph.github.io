3. advantage of non woven fabric mask​


NON-WOVEN FABRIC MASK

Although medical-grade nonwoven polypropylene (NWPP) (20–25 gsm) is used in commercial masks and respirators, these materials are currently reserved for manufacturers who best meet medical, legal, and regulatory standards for ethical reasons. Given that these medical grade NWPP materials are not readily available to the general people at this time, reusable supermarket bags (70 – 100 gsm) have been the chosen sources of spunbond NWPP for use in the community masks. However, NWPP from recycled supermarket bags is not widely available by the yard to sewists and mask manufacturers. As a result, spunbond NWPP that is more generally accessible by the yard (i.e., used in apparel, textiles, handicraft, and upholstery applications) is being researched for applicability in masks.

1. Breathability - Although research into the ideal number of NWPP layers is continuing, preliminary laboratory findings indicate that three layers of spunbond NWPP provide acceptable breathability for fitted and non-fitting mask designs. Despite qualitative evaluations to the contrary, masks with extra layers began to indicate performance declines (e.g., increased pressure differential across the masks and suboptimal CO2 accumulation). The appropriate number of NWPP layers will vary based on the unique materials used as well as the trade-off between usability, breathability, particle filtration, and planned period of usage.

2. Droplet Resistance - Fluid resistance helps reduce potential exposure to blood and body fluids caused by splashes, spray, or spatter [Infection Control Today, 2003]." Fluid resistance is measured for FDA-regulated medical masks as the "Resistance to Penetration by Synthetic Blood...as specified in Test Method F 1862." Although this test is performed using synthetic blood, it is commonly used to establish the fluid resistance of masks such as those used for droplet protection.

3. Disinfection - Additional disinfection strategies have been proposed, although not all of them have been studied for this purpose. Harsh chemicals (e.g., bleach) are not suggested in general because leftover chemicals in the mask may pose unintentional inhalation hazards. Furthermore, oxidizing chemicals may cause the mask materials to degrade faster.
