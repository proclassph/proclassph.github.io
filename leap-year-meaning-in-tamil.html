11. what is a leap year?​


What is a leap year?

A leap year is a year that contains an additional day, February 29th, to keep the calendar year synchronized with the astronomical or seasonal year. This is because the Earth's orbit around the Sun is not exactly 365 days long, but is actually about 365.24 days long. In order to account for this extra time, every four years an extra day is added to the calendar year, making it 366 days long instead of the usual 365 days.

However, this extra day is not added every four years uniformly. According to the Gregorian calendar, which is the most widely used calendar system today, a year is a leap year if it is divisible by 4, except for years that are divisible by 100 but not divisible by 400. For example, the year 1900 was not a leap year because although it was divisible by 4, it was also divisible by 100 but not by 400. However, the year 2000 was a leap year because it was divisible by both 4 and 400.

Leap years are important for keeping the calendar year aligned with the astronomical year and ensuring that seasonal events, such as the solstices and equinoxes, occur at roughly the same time each year.

Answer:

Leap year is a calendar year that contains an additional day added to keep the calendar year synchronized with the astronomical year or seasonal year. It is also the year (every four years) with 366 days instead of 365 days.
