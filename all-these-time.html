Again, 0 cannot be first, so remove it. Since the number must be odd, it must end in either 1 or 3.  Place 1, then, in the last position.  _ _ _ _ 1.  Therefore, for the first position, we may choose either 2, 3, or 4, so that there are 3 ways to choose the first digit.  Now replace 0.  Hence, there will be 3 ways to choose the second position, 2 ways to choose the third, and 1 way to choose the fourth. Therefore, the total number of odd numbers that end in 1, is 3· 3· 2· 1 = 18.  The same analysis holds if we place 3 in the last position, so that the total number of odd numbers is 2· 18 = 36.

Once upon a time, there was a kingdom ruled by a mighty leader. This leader had a son whom he wished to succeed him at some point in the future. Wanting to prepare the son for leadership, the king summoned his wisest advisers.

“I want you to collect and assemble all the wisdom of the world,” he commanded, “such that my son, when he ascends to the throne, will be capable of attaining the wisdom and knowledge he will need to succeed.”

The wise men bowed and left the king’s court and set about to collect and scribe the knowledge of the world in many volumes. After a few years of effort, they presented to the king a stack of 20 large volumes, filled with the council, advice, knowledge, and wisdom collected from all over the known world. However, the king was displeased with the work.

“This effort, while noble,” he began, “is far too challenging. My son, indeed few men, could learn, digest and implement such a large body of work. You must summarize the information.”

And so the wise men went out again to summarize the body of work into something valuable yet much easier to digest by the maturing prince. After quite some effort and time, they returned to the king again. The leader of the wise men presented to the king one slim volume of text and illustration, the summary of all the world’s important data and knowledge.

And yet the king was still dissatisfied.

“An impressive work to be sure,” the king granted, “but the time to develop this volume has been so excessive that time grows short for me and my son will be taking over soon I fear. You must yet come up with something even more palatable by a young king.”

The wise men left again and worked diligently through the nights to summarize the important information yet again, and after some time, appeared to the king with a small scrap of paper, presenting it to him with a flourish.

“Upon this parchment,” the wise man said, is a summation of the summary of all the knowledge of the world that a young king may need.”

The king eagerly snatched the document and read it. Written on the page was the following sentence:

“This, too, shall pass.”


19. "health care is vital to all of us some of the time, but public health is vital to all of us all of the time." -c.Everette koop8​


Answer:

The moral theory known as “ the ethics of care” implies that there is moral significance in the fundamental elements of relationships and dependencies in human life. Normatively, care ethics seeks to maintain relationships by contextualizing and promoting the well-being of care-givers and care-receivers in a network of social relations. Most often defined as a practice or virtue rather than a theory as such, “care” involves maintaining the world of, and meeting the needs of, ourself and others. It builds on the motivation to care for those who are dependent and vulnerable, and it is inspired by both memories of being cared for and the idealizations of self. Following in the sentimentalist tradition of moral theory, care ethics affirms the importance of caring motivation, emotion and the body in moral deliberation, as well as reasoning from particulars. One of the original works of care ethics was Milton Mayeroff’s short book, On Caring, but the emergence of care ethics as a distinct moral theory is most often attributed to the works of psychologist Carol Gilligan and philosopher Nel Noddings in the mid-1980s. Both charged traditional moral approaches with male bias, and asserted the “voice of care” as a legitimate alternative to the “justice perspective” of liberal human rights theory. Annette Baier, Virginia Held, Eva Feder Kittay, Sara Ruddick, and Joan Tronto are some of the most influential among many subsequent contributors to care ethics.
