11. Is true that social and natural systems are separable?​


“Is it true that social and natural systems are separable?”

Despite having many inter-links between social and natural systems, a wide variety of arguments have been advanced to suggest that the two are fundamentally separate. Inherent in these arguments are a number of philosophical, scientific, and practical considerations. A closer look at these issues reveals a more balanced view of the relationship between them.

First, from a philosophical perspective, the idea of social and natural systems being separate is not a new one. Many theorists such as Descartes and Kant postulate that the social world is separate and distinct from the natural world. They posit that the natural world is governed by physical laws, whereas the social world is shaped by intangible forces such as values, beliefs and cultures. Therefore, they argued that the two systems remain irreconcilable. Nevertheless, other philosophers, such as John Stuart Mill, contested this notion by affirming that many of the laws and principles of the natural world also apply to the social realm. This argument has served to blur the distinction between the two domains.

From a scientific viewpoint, the relationship between social and natural systems is even more complicated. Many scientists argue that the two systems exist in an interactive relationship with one another. On one hand, natural systems can have a direct and powerful influence on the development and maintenance of the social systems. For instance, changes in the climate can have a significant impact on a society's economy and food production. On the other hand, social systems can also have an indirect effect on the natural world by manipulating and using natural resources to fulfill their needs. As a result, ecosystems can be altered or destroyed by humans. For instance, deforestation is a classic example of the destructive power of social systems on natural systems.

At the same time, other scientists argue that, although social and natural systems are inextricably connected, the two remain distinct entities. Specifically, they suggest that the fundamental activities and objectives of the two systems are different, and thus must remain apart. They further argue that phenomena and processes in the natural world must be observed and understood from a scientific perspective, while those in the social world must be analyzed from a social and ethical point of view. In other words, even though there is an inter-link between the two systems, from a scientific vantage point, social and natural systems must be treated differently.

Finally, from a practical standpoint, many argue that separating social and natural systems is beneficial because it helps us to better understand and address their respective challenges. For example, scientists can focus on understanding and managing the dynamics of the natural world without being distracted by the complexities of social issues. Likewise, social scientists can concentrate on understanding and handling social problems without having to grapple with the subtle nuances of the natural environment. In conclusion, while social and natural systems remain connected, there is a strong argument to suggest that the two must remain distinct entities. This argument is bolstered by philosophical, scientific, and practical considerations.
